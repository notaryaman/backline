Project Summary

Backline reframes frontline support as a recovery problem, not just a training problem.

Retail fulfillment associates are expected to move quickly, stay accurate, and stay calm in public-facing environments. Our project began with a broad interest in stressful service work, but narrowed into timer-driven retail fulfillment after we found that the hardest moments were not routine task steps. The real breakdowns happened when an item was missing, when a customer interruption broke context, or when asking for help felt socially costly.

Backline is a shift support tool that addresses those moments directly. Instead of adding another passive training layer, it provides just-in-time guidance for missing-item recovery, contextual escalation, and interruption recovery. Across the course, we used needfinding, POV development, experience prototyping, low-fidelity exploration, medium-fidelity interaction design, and high-fidelity refinement to move from a broad opportunity space to a focused support concept for live work.

Problem And Opportunity

Formal training covers official workflow, but not judgment under pressure.

Our early research showed a recurring mismatch between how retail fulfillment is taught and how it is actually performed. Workers are trained on scanner mechanics, order flow, and official procedure, yet real performance depends on how they respond when the plan breaks. Missing items, risky substitutions, customer interruptions, and uncertainty about whether to escalate all create decision-making pressure that is mostly invisible to formal training systems.

We also found that speed metrics can distort behavior. Workers are measured on pace, but speed alone is not a full picture of competent performance. Under timer pressure, workers sometimes optimize for completion at the cost of accuracy, confidence, or customer experience. This suggested a design opportunity: create a support system that helps workers recover quickly and accurately during live breakdown moments without making them feel slowed down or incompetent.

Needfinding

We narrowed from broad service work to timer-driven retail fulfillment.

We conducted semi-structured interviews and used synthesis methods such as empathy maps and cross-interview pattern analysis to identify recurring tensions. By GR2, the project had narrowed to retail fulfillment, with one newer picker and one experienced fulfillment lead serving as the core interview perspectives represented in our website and deliverable materials.

Training taught mechanics, not judgment.

Workers described learning official procedure, but not the practical heuristics needed to make fast decisions when ambiguity appeared on the floor.

Help-seeking felt both operationally and socially costly.

Asking a supervisor for help could reduce pressure, but it also risked making the worker feel slow, dependent, or visibly unsure.

Interruptions created real context loss.

When workers were pulled away to help customers, they often had to reconstruct the order from memory, especially in time-sensitive sections.

Experience functioned like hidden expertise.

Experienced workers knew unofficial store patterns such as likely substitute locations, common shelf errors, and escalation timing.

POVs And Design Questions

We framed the opportunity around confidence, pace, and recovery.

The strongest POV we carried forward focused on a newer retail fulfillment associate who wants to keep pace and look competent, but needs fast support for ambiguous edge-case decisions because formal training covers official process rather than real performance under pressure.

How might we help associates recover quickly from missing-item decisions without sacrificing timer performance?
How might we make asking a supervisor for help feel fast and competent instead of embarrassing?
How might we help workers resume a task after a customer interruption without losing context or confidence?

Concept Exploration

We explored multiple modalities before committing to a phone-first direction.

In our low-fidelity work, we compared several realizations of the same core idea. The most promising options were a phone app for live shift support, a smartwatch companion for glanceable interruption and escalation support, and a cart-mounted display that could provide visible guidance during picking.

We selected the phone-first direction because it offered the best balance between feasibility, information density, and support for multi-step recovery flows. The watch concept remained valuable as an inspiration for glanceable interactions, but the phone gave us enough space to combine decision support, escalation, and resume context in a coherent medium-fidelity and high-fidelity experience.

Task Set

Three tasks anchored the design process from GR2 through GR10.

Simple

Resolve a missing item during an active pick run.

This task captures a frequent uncertainty-heavy moment and tests whether support can be nearly frictionless.

Moderate

Pause for a customer interruption and resume the order with context intact.

This task focuses on context switching and memory recovery rather than a single isolated decision.

Complex

Handle an at-risk order with multiple missing items, request supervisor help, and complete the run.

This task combines uncertainty, escalation, multiple decisions, and timer pressure in one end-to-end flow.

Design Evolution

Each prototype pass reduced ambiguity in the highest-pressure moments.

Our design process moved from broad service-work support toward targeted recovery support for retail fulfillment. The low-fidelity phase helped us compare modalities and surface the tradeoff between glanceability and information density. It also forced us to define the three canonical tasks more clearly.

The medium-fidelity phase focused on structure. We merged guidance and action on the same screen so users did not have to bounce across multiple views to understand what to do. We added a persistent task context header so interruptions would not erase the user’s place in the order. We also converted generic help-seeking into structured escalation cards with auto-filled context, reducing the burden of explaining the problem under pressure.

The high-fidelity phase then refined hierarchy, clarity, and confidence. Primary actions became more distinct from secondary actions. Confirmation states became more visible so users would know when a recommendation or recovery step had actually been applied. Overall, the design direction became less about generic training and more about guided recovery in the exact moment of uncertainty.

Final Solution

Backline supports three breakdown moments: missing items, escalation, and interruption recovery.

Missing-item recovery

Backline offers alternate-location hints, substitute guidance, and unavailable-item fallbacks so workers can choose the fastest safe next step without over-searching.

Contextual escalation

Escalation requests automatically bundle item, aisle, timer state, and issue so that asking for help feels efficient rather than competence-threatening.

Interruption resume

Resume cards preserve order state, next item, and section context so users can return to the right task after helping a customer.

Prototype Scope

The final prototype uses hard-coded sample data to support the target tasks.

The prototype does not include backend integration or live store data. Instead, it uses hard-coded sample orders, inventory states, escalation responses, and timer states so users can complete the target flows without additional infrastructure. This scope let us concentrate on interaction design rather than implementation complexity.

These limitations are acceptable for a course prototype because the core question is not whether the system can connect to a store backend, but whether the interaction model makes recovery feel faster, clearer, and more competent in the moment.

AI Tools Used

AI was used for writing support and structure, not for design evidence.

We used AI assistance to help organize written material and draft presentation-quality copy. We did not use AI to fabricate participant data, heuristic-evaluation results, usability findings, or any other empirical design evidence.

Reflection

Designing for frontline work required treating recovery as a first-class interaction.

One of the most important lessons from this project was that frontline work problems are not only efficiency problems. They are also confidence, coordination, and context-recovery problems. Missing items and interruptions look like small exceptions from the outside, but for workers they are often the moments where performance, emotion, and customer service collide.

Backline emerged from that insight. Rather than assuming workers simply needed more training, the project focused on supporting the exact moments where knowledge breaks down under pressure. If we continued this project, the next step would be broader real-world evaluation across different store environments and a closer look at how supervisor-facing tooling could complement the worker-facing flows.
